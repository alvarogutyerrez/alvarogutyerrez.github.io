Testing on the (unfortunately) boundary of the maintained Hypothesis

Hypothesis testing

In standard testing problems, parameter values in the null hypothesis are interior points of the maintained hypothesis. (D.K.Andrews, 2001) The standard testing problema happens to be as follows being \(\beta_{1}\) a sub-vector of the unknown parameter \(\theta\). \[ \begin{align} H_{0}:&&\beta_{1}=\beta_{1*} && (\text{Null Hypotheses} ) \\ H_{1}:&&\beta_{1}\neq\beta_{1*} && (\text{Alternative Hypotheses} ) \\ K:&&\beta_{1}\in R^{p_{1}} && (\text{Maintained Hypotheses} ) \\ \text{where } && K = H_{0} \cup H_{1} \end{align} \] Additionally, all parameters are identified under the null and the alternative hypotheses.

The main article of D.K.Andrews, (2001) works both with GARCH models and Random Coefficients or Mixed models. In the following I will just address the former case, given that is the one that I familiar with.

We can argue that the regularity conditions stated above fail in the context of random coefficients given the following. Let try to test the null hypothesis that \(b\) random coefficients have variances equal to zero. If we denote \(\beta_{1}\) as the sum of the \(b\) random coefficients variances that are specified to be zero under the null hyphothesis. The null, alternative, and maintained hypotheses are as follow:

\[ \begin{align} H_{0}:&&\beta_{1}=0 && (\text{Null Hypotheses} ) \\ H_{1}:&&\beta_{1}>0 && (\text{Alternative Hypotheses} ) \\ K:&&\beta_{1}\geq 0 && (\text{Maintained Hypotheses} ) \end{align} \] Consequently, we can see that, under the null hyphothesis \(H_0\), \(\beta_{1}\) is on the boundry of the maintained hypothesis. Therefore, we are in troubles….

Additionally, suppose that some or all the \(b\) random coefficients whose variances are under test are allowed to be correlated with each other under \(H_{0}\). Denoting \(\pi_{1}\) the vector containing the correlation coefficients. Denote the \(b\) vector of random coefficients variances that are under test by \(\beta_{1}\pi_{2}\). Here, \(\pi_{2}\) is unit \(b\) vector in the nonnegative orthant. Let \(\pi = (\pi^{'}_{1},\pi^{'}_{2})'\)

Here is the problem: Under the alternative hypothesis, \(\beta_{1}\) and \(\pi\) specify the variances and covariances of the random coefficients under test, BUT under the NULL Hypothesis, the parameter \(\pi\) is not identified.

The Method:

Obtaining the asymptotic null distribution of the test statistics

\(\mathscr{I}_{T}(\theta, \pi)\), where \((\theta, \pi)\) are parameters that lie in the maintained hypothesis paramter space \(\Theta \times \Pi\) and \(T\) denotes the sample size.

The null hypothesis can be written as \(H_{0}:\theta\in \Theta_{0}\), where \(\Theta_{0}\) is a subset of \(\Theta\).

The basic idea is:

  1. Appoximate \(\mathscr{I}_{T}(\theta, \pi)\) by a quadratic function of \(\theta\) whose coefficients depend on \(\pi\)

  2. Shows that the approximation holds uniformly over \(\pi\in\Pi\).

  3. Approximate the unrestricted and restricted parameter spaces, \(\Theta\) and \(\Theta_{0}\), by cones, as in Chernoff (1954). and Andrews (1999).

  4. Determine the asymptotic distributions of the (Suitably normalized) unrestricted and restricted estimators of \(\theta\) given \(\pi\) and of the estimator objective function evaluated at these two estimators as stochastic processes indexed by \(\pi\in\Pi\)

  5. Obtain the asymptotic null distributions of the test statistics by writing them as continuous functions of the (normalized) estimators and/or the objective function evaluated at these estimators and applying the continuous mapping theorem.
